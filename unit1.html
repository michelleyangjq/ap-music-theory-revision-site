Music Fundamentals I

Pitch, Scales, and Key Signatures

In this unit, we will review the different clefs, scales, and key signatures. We will review how to read the four primary clefs and how to find the key signatures of scales and vice versa.

Clefs: Treble, Bass, Alto, Tenor

When musicians notate music, they need to determine how they can express pitch - or how high or low a note is. To this end, musicians work with different clefs, a symbol that is placed at the beginning of the music, giving us a reference for interpreting pitch.


AP Music Theory works with four main clefs (note: lines are counted from bottom up):

  1. Treble (G Clef): Marks the second line as G4 and is used for higher-pitched instruments like the violin, flute and the right hand of a piano. The lines of the treble clef spell out E G B D F (from bottom to top), which can be memorized using "Every Good Boy Does Fine." The spaces spell out FACE.
  2. Bass (F Clef): Marks the fourth line as F3 and is used for lower-pitched instruments like the trombone, basson, and generally the left hand of piano. The lines of the bass clef spell out G B D F A ("Good Boys Do Fine Always") and the spaces spell out A C E G.
  3. Alto (C Clef): Marks middle C (C4) on the third line. Used for alto (middle-pitched) instruments like the viola. The lines of the alto clef spell out F A C E G and the spaces spell out G B D F.
  4. Tenor (C Clef): Middle C (C4) on the fourth line. Used for tenor (middle-but-lower-pitched) instruments like the cello. The lines of the alto clef spell out F A C E G and the spaces spell out G B D F.

❓: WAIT! I thought the alto clef was the C Clef?
👩‍💻: Both the alto and tenor clef are part of the C Clef Family, which consists of all clefs whos pointy signals to Middle C! Similarly, treble clefs are G Clefs because they signal where G is, while bass clefs are F Clefs because they signal where F is!


To do well on the test, it is important to be fluent with the treble and bass clef, and be able to interpret the alto and tenor clef at a reasonable speed.

Q: Which clef would an oboe most likely use?

Show Answer

An oboe is a high-pitched instrument, so it uses the treble clef.

Keys

When we move in pitches, we move in steps: half-steps or whole-steps. A whole-step is equivalent to two half-steps. A half-step would be the distance from C to C#, whereas a whole-step would be moving from C to D. However, remember that the distance from E-F and B-C are half-steps.

Key Terms & Definitions

Treble Clef (G Clef)
Used for higher-pitched instruments.
Lines: E G B D F — "Every Good Boy Does Fine." Spaces: F A C E.
Bass Clef (F Clef)
Used for lower-pitched instruments.
Lines: G B D F A — "Good Boys Do Fine Always." Spaces: A C E G.
Alto Clef (C Clef)
Middle C (C4) on the third line. Used for alto (middle-pitched) instruments like the viola.
Tenor Clef (C Clef)
Middle C (C4) on the fourth line. Used for tenor (middle-but-lower-pitched) instruments like the cello.

Ledger Lines
The lines on a staff
Time Signature
Two numbers at the beginning of a piece. The top number = beats per measure. The bottom number = which note value gets one beat (4 = quarter note, 8 = eighth note, 2 = half note).
Simple Meter
Each beat divides into two equal parts. Examples: 2/4, 3/4, 4/4, 2/2, 3/2. The top number is 2, 3, or 4.
Compound Meter
Each beat divides into three equal parts. Examples: 6/8, 9/8, 12/8, 6/4. The top number is 6, 9, or 12. Divide top by 3 to get the number of beats.
Dotted Note
A dot after a note increases its duration by half. A dotted quarter = quarter + eighth = 1.5 beats (in 4/4). A double dot adds half of the first dot as well.
Tie vs. Slur
A tie connects two notes of the same pitch — held, not re-attacked. A slur connects notes of different pitches — play smoothly (legato).
Tuplet
A group of notes that divides a beat in an unusual way. A triplet (3 notes) fills the space of 2. A duplet (2 notes) fills the space of 3 in compound meter.
Syncopation
Accenting a weak beat or the "and" of a beat — shifting the expected rhythmic emphasis. Creates a sense of rhythmic tension.
Hemiola
A rhythmic device where two groups of three (or three groups of two) are implied within the same metric space. Common in Baroque and Renaissance music.

Practice Problem

Q: In 6/8 time, how many eighth notes are in one measure, and how many beats are there?

Show Answer

6/8 has 6 eighth notes per measure but only 2 beats (compound duple). Each beat = a dotted quarter note, which is divided into 3 eighth notes. Count as: "1-and-a, 2-and-a."
