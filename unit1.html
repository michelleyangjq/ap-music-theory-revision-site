Music Fundamentals I

Pitch, Scales, and Key Signatures

In this unit, we will review the different clefs, scales, and key signatures. We will review how to read the four primary clefs and how to find the key signatures of scales and vice versa.

Helpful "cheatsheet" charts are included at the end of the page.

Clefs: Treble, Bass, Alto, Tenor

When musicians notate music, they need to determine how they can express pitch - or how high or low a note is. To this end, musicians work with different clefs, a symbol that is placed at the beginning of the music, giving us a reference for interpreting pitch.

AP Music Theory works with four main clefs (note: lines are counted from bottom up):

  1. Treble (G Clef): Marks the second line as G4 and is used for higher-pitched instruments like the violin, flute and the right hand of a piano. The lines of the treble clef spell out E G B D F (from bottom to top), which can be memorized using "Every Good Boy Does Fine." The spaces spell out FACE.
  2. Bass (F Clef): Marks the fourth line as F3 and is used for lower-pitched instruments like the trombone, basson, and generally the left hand of piano. The lines of the bass clef spell out G B D F A ("Good Boys Do Fine Always") and the spaces spell out A C E G.
  3. Alto (C Clef): Marks middle C (C4) on the third line. Used for alto (middle-pitched) instruments like the viola. The lines of the alto clef spell out F A C E G and the spaces spell out G B D F.
  4. Tenor (C Clef): Middle C (C4) on the fourth line. Used for tenor (middle-but-lower-pitched) instruments like the cello. The lines of the tenor clef spell out F A C E G and the spaces spell out G B D F.

❓: WAIT! I thought the alto clef was the C Clef?
👩‍💻: Both the alto and tenor clef are part of the C Clef Family, which consists of all clefs whos pointy signals to Middle C! Similarly, treble clefs are G Clefs because they signal where G is, while bass clefs are F Clefs because they signal where F is!

To do well on the test, it is important to be fluent with the treble and bass clef, and be able to interpret the alto and tenor clef at a reasonable speed.

Q: Which clef would an oboe use?

Show Answer

An oboe is a high-pitched instrument, so it uses the treble clef.

Steps and Scales

When we move in pitches, we move in steps: half-steps or whole-steps. A whole-step is equivalent to two half-steps. A half-step would be the distance from C to C#, whereas a whole-step would be moving from C to D. However, remember that the distance from E-F and B-C are half-steps.

When we organize several notes together in ascending order, we create a scale.

Major Scales

A Major Scale is a scale with seven notes with five whole-steps and two half-steps (between the third and fourth note and the seventh and eight note - the eight note is the same as the first note!).

When refer to the notes in a major scale by its scale degree: Scale degree one is the first note of the scale, scale degree two the second, scale degree three the third, and so on! Apart from "scale degree x", we also have more "formal" terms for the notes:

  1. Tonic
  2. Supertonic
  3. Mediant
  4. Subdominant
  5. Dominant
  6. Submediant
  7. Leading Tone

👩‍💻: A lot of people get confused as to why scale degree 6 is the "submediant". Think of it like this:

The mediant is two notes above the tonic, so the submediant is two notes below the tonic.

Q: If a note were the subdominant of a key, it would be scale degree ___.

Show Answer

The subdominant of the key refers to scale degree 4.

Keys and Key Signatures

A piece of music has a key when it has a tonal center, or a note that is like the "main character." To establish a key, we use key signatures to indicate which notes are included in this key. A key tells us about the key signature, and the key signature can also tell us about the key.

A key signature would include sharps (♯), flats (♭), or neither. Sharp keys will always appear in the order: FCGDAEB, meaning, if a key has one sharp, it must be F♯; a key signature with two sharps must have F♯ and C♯; and so on... Flat keys will always appear in the reverse order: BEADGCF.

👩‍💻 Tip: Memorize the orders as if they were one single word: fadosorelamiti and timilaresodofa

Q: If a note were the subdominant of a key, it would be scale degree ___.

Show Answer

Take the first four letters of the sequence BEADGCF - The four flats are B♭, E♭, A♭, and D♭.

Rhythm

Apart from pitch, music is also concerned with rhythm. While pitch answers what note is being played, rhythm answers when the note is being played.

Additionally, a dot increases a note's value by 50%, while a tie sums the duration of two identical notes into one note with a longer duration.

👩‍💻: A tie across multiple different notes is called a slur, which means to play these notes smoothly.

Q: How many beats should you play if you see two quarter notes tied together?

Show Answer

1 + 1 = 2 beats.

Q: How many beats does a dotted whole note have?

Show Answer

A whole note has 4 beats, so a dotted whole note would have 4 + 2 = 6 beats since 50% of 4 is 2.

Challenge Question: How many beats does a dotted dotted whole note have?

Show Answer

The dot of a dotted whole note has 2 beats, so the dot after it has a duration of 1 beat. In total, a dotted dotted half note is 7 beats.

Meter

Meter refers to the way rhythm is grouped and felt. When you clap to a piece of music and feel 1-2-3-4, that's feeling the music's meter!

Meter is grouped into individual measures, its pattern determined by the piece's time signature, found at the beginning of the staff. A time signature always has two numbers: The number on the top tells us how many beats we have per measure, while the number on the bottom tells us which note get's the beat - In other words, what unit we are counting in.

E.g. #1: In 3/4 time, we have three beats per measure, while we use quarter notes as our unit. Thus, every measure's duration would equate to three quarter notes.

E.g. #2: In 2/2 time (or cut-time), we have two beats per measure, but we use half notes as our unit -- Every measure's duration would equate to two half notes.

👩‍💻: A lot of people wonder why 2/2 isn't equal to 4/4 in music. The answer is because we perceive these two differently. A piece in 4/4 is generally faster, with four distinct pulses. On the other hand, 2/2 pieces are often slower, have bigger lines, and only feel like they have two main beats. It's a lot about what it feels like.

Q: If we are in 6/8 time, how many beats does each measure have? Which note gets the beat?

Show Answer

There are six notes per measure, and the eight note gets the beat.

Types of Meter

When we describe meter, we focus on two categories:

  1. Is it simple or compound?
  2. Is it duple, triple, or quadruple?

Simple VS Compound

When you feel like a single beat can be naturally divided into two, four, eight, or so on, the piece is likely in simple time. Compound time, however, feels like it wants to get divided into three.
Here are some examples of what simple VS compound time sounds like:

  1. Simple: Beauty and the Beast, 1:01
  2. Compound: Into the Unknown 0:15

Generally, time signatures with 8 on the bottom are compound, with 3/8 being an exception.

Duple, Triple, or Quadruple

This simply means "how many beats are in a measure". In duple time, we feel two distinct pulses; in triple, three; and in quadruple, four pulses.

In simple time, the top number of a key signature would tell you quite straightforwardly how many beats there are. For instance, 3/4 is triple time. It is slightly trickier in compound time: You would have to divide the top number by three. For example, 9/8 time is also triple time since 9/3 = 3.

When we describe a time signature, we evaluate it with both criterions. Hence, 3/4 would be simple triple time, 9/8 would be compound triple time, 12/8 would be compound quadruple time, etc.

Q: How would you describe 4/4 time?

Show Answer

Simple quadruple time.

Q: How would you describe 6/8 time?

Show Answer

Compound duple time.

Tempo

Lastly, tempo refers to how fast a piece is. Classical composers often used italian expressions that indicated an interval of tempo because these expressions simultaneously conveyed the emotions of the piece. Now, exact metronome markings are more common.

While you don't have to have the exact range of every tempo marking memorized for the exam, you would need to have a general basic understanding of them. For example, although you don't have to know what the range for adagio is, you should know that it would be much slower than vivace.

Key Terms & Definitions

Treble Clef (G Clef)
Used for higher-pitched instruments.
Lines: E G B D F — "Every Good Boy Does Fine." Spaces: F A C E.
Bass Clef (F Clef)
Used for lower-pitched instruments.
Lines: G B D F A — "Good Boys Do Fine Always." Spaces: A C E G.
Alto Clef (C Clef)
Middle C (C4) on the third line. Used for alto (middle-pitched) instruments like the viola.
Tenor Clef (C Clef)
Middle C (C4) on the fourth line. Used for tenor (middle-but-lower-pitched) instruments like the cello.
Dot
Adds 50% to a note's duration.
Tie
Sums the duration of two or more notes.
Time Signature
Two numbers at the beginning of a piece. The top number = beats per measure. The bottom number = which note value gets one beat.
Simple Meter
Each beat divides into two equal parts. Examples: 2/4, 3/4, 4/4, 2/2, 3/2.
Compound Meter
Each beat divides into three equal parts. Examples: 6/8, 9/8, 12/8, 6/4.
Duple
Two beats.
Triple
Three beats.
Quadruple
Two beats.
Tempo
The speed of a piece.
